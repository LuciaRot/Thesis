Level Design

Pre-production

Now that you have planned the core of your game it is time to take on the levels.

There are a lot of different types of levels:

  • Clearly marked levels (i.e. Candy Crush)
  • Player levels (i.e. Genshin Impact)
  • Skill levels (i.e. Stardew Valley)
  • Boss fights (i.e. Elden Ring)
  • Different Maps (i.e. Valorant)

    • Can you think of more examples?

    "Level design is the practice of planning and building spaces for video games" The Level Design Book ¹

    An important thought when building your levels is how you're going to lead the player to do specific things.
    In order to do that you have to know what the goal of the level is. Is the player supposed to find a collectible? Are they supposed to meet an NPC? Define your goals and then think about how you get the player to achieve them.

    In the process the information below can help you to define your goals:

    Experience goals

    While planning your game you have defined some experience goals you want to achieve.
    Build your level in a way that contributes to that.

    Pacing

    Pacing is the general order and rhythm of activities and events in a level. This can also be used to shape the experience. Do you want to make the player feel stressed in a certain level? Make a lot of things happen in a short amount of time or set a time limit for a task.

    Hierarchy

    Which parts of the level are the most important?

    Causality

    Why does the player do this before that?

    Information

    What do you tell the player and when?

    Intensity

    When should the player pay more attention and when do they rest and recover?

    Mission Design

    Mission Design is often confused with level design. They are different though. One level can contain multiple missions.

    If you want to level up your character you first need to collect the neccessary items. These might be obtained through combat or exporing. When the player completed all missions neccessary they can complete the level.

    Dead by Daylight - BHVR Interactive, 2016

    In the game "Dead by Daylight" the goal of the level is to escape the level through the exit gates on the map. In order to achieve that goal the players have to complete multiple missions first. They have to repair generators, unhook their team mates, heal each other, distract the killer and open the exit gates. The level goal can not be achieved without completing the missions.

    Check out Ubisoft Toronto Next. It's a game design contest for students. On the website you can find the briefings for the different categories and what the winners ended up doing. This can help you to get a feeling for tasks that you might get in the industry.

    Layout

    When you are done planning your level you can start with the layout. The layout is the basic structure of the level. To visualize this, a 2D skech from above is usually used.

    Create a Layout for a level in your game.

    Flow

    The flow is the way a player moves through a level. The best flow is whatever supports the intended design goals. This involves parameters like the speed at which a player can move, the direction (Is the path straight or curvy etc.), wayfinding (does the player have to find their way or is it clearly marked?) and metrics (What are the movement mechanics?).

    Desire Lines and Critical pathing

    The desire line is tha path that a player intuitively wants to take through a level. Often times these are the shortest paths to a specific destination. If these are blocked by something it might feel frustrating to the player because taking the longer way can feel like a waste of time. You can also use this as a tool to achieve your experience goals.

    The critical path is the minimum/main path to complete a level. This is the minimum distance the player has to cover to successfully go through the level.
